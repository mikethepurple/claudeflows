# Build in Public — Research Report

**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Context:** ClaudeFlows redesign — evaluating "build in public" as a content-to-customer marketing channel for Misha's projects (vibe-coded with Claude Code)

---

## Key Findings

1. **It works, but the timeline is years, not weeks.** The dominant model is compounding — Pieter Levels spent 10 years building 600K followers before his biggest product ($132K MRR) launched. But AI-era founders are compressing this: Jack Friks built a couples app in 5 days, got 500K tweet views, made first dollar in 24 hours — zero hand-typed code.

2. **Revenue transparency is the highest-engagement content type.** Exact MRR numbers ("$132,487") outperform ranges. Stripe dashboard screenshots go viral. The mechanism is social proof + relatable aspiration.

3. **The funnel is: audience → trust → launch → instant revenue.** Pieter Levels got $5,400 in week one from Photo AI because 350K Twitter followers already trusted him. Unknown founders launching cold typically get $500-2K in the same window. Build in public is pre-selling at scale without running ads.

4. **Twitter/X is the primary channel; everything else is secondary.** LinkedIn works for professional verticals. IndieHackers and WIP.co are community launchers, not audience builders. Newsletter converts but Twitter builds the top of funnel.

5. **Revenue transparency has a natural lifecycle.** 0–$10K MRR: share everything. $10K–$30K: go quiet on numbers, share product lessons. $30K+: selective milestones only. Copycats arrive at scale but rarely succeed — the real threat is psychological stress, not lost market share.

6. **The "I built X with AI" format is accelerating.** Jack Friks (Claude + Cursor), Marc Lou (AI launch videos), and Danny Postma (30-day AI builds) are all getting outsized engagement by pairing speed claims with AI tools. The format is: "I had zero [skill/code/design]. I used AI. Here's what happened." This is directly applicable to Misha's workflow.

7. **Anti-patterns are mostly about inconsistency and missing the CTA.** Sharing wins only (fake transparency), posting sporadically, never linking to the product, and chasing vanity followers instead of ICP followers are the top failure modes.

### Twitter/X Thread Formats That Drive Engagement

**Highest-performing formats (in order of engagement):**

1. **The milestone reveal** — "We crossed $X MRR" + Stripe screenshot. Exact numbers massively outperform ranges. Triggers reshares from other founders and aspiration from audience.

2. **The speed build** — "I built X in Y days/hours using AI." Engagement spikes when paired with specific tool names (Claude, Cursor, v0). Jack Friks's couples app post: 500K views.

3. **The failure thread** — "I spent $X on ads and got Y signups" or "Why my product failed." Counterintuitively higher engagement than wins because it's relatable and educational. Saves and reshares as reference material.

4. **The teardown thread** — "Here's how [successful product] actually works under the hood." Provides value to developers and founders while establishing technical credibility.

5. **The breadcrumb series** — Daily "shipping" updates (WIP-style). Low engagement per post, high cumulative effect — builds a narrative of momentum.

**Thread anatomy that works:**
- Hook tweet: bold claim or number (avoid question hooks)
- Tweets 2-4: the tension and backstory
- Tweets 5-7: the revelation/lesson with specific data
- Final tweet: clear CTA (product link, newsletter signup)
- Visual breaks every 3-4 tweets with screenshots or charts → 45% higher completion rate

**What kills engagement:**
- Sharing only wins (perceived as fake/polished)
- Asking "thoughts?" as the hook
- Threading updates without linking to the product
- Inconsistent posting (algorithm penalizes dormancy heavily)

### YouTube Build-in-Public Format

**Primary format:** "I built [product] in [time] with [AI tool]" — combines the appeal of speedrun content with the maker community's interest in AI tools.

**Revenue mechanics for developer YouTube:**
- CPM for developer/tech audience: $10-15 (high, because developer tools advertisers pay well)
- 1M views = $10K-15K ad revenue at standard CPM
- But the real revenue is product adoption and consulting leads
- Theo's model: YouTube drives stack adoption → ecosystem tools become monetization

**Typical view counts for niche tech build channels:**
- First 10 videos: 500-5K views each
- First viral video: 50K-500K (when "I built X with AI" format lands)
- Consistent channels: 10K-50K per video at 50K-200K subscriber tier

**The conversion path:** YouTube → Twitter → product → word of mouth. YouTube rarely converts directly; it builds the top of a trust funnel that closes on Twitter/X.

### Newsletter Build-in-Public

**How it works as a conversion channel:**

Tony Dinh's newsletter sends monthly MRR updates to subscribers → keeps warm audience engaged between launches → product announcements go to a list that's been primed.

The newsletter-to-product pipeline: free content → email capture → monthly updates with honest numbers → launch announcement to warm list → conversion.

**Benchmarks:**
- Indie hacker newsletters with 5K-15K subscribers at 40%+ open rates convert 2-8% of list on a new product launch
- Primary value: retention and re-engagement, not top-of-funnel acquisition

**Paid newsletter model (Arvid Kahl / Bootstrapped Founder):**
- Build in public content → newsletter grows → newsletter itself becomes the product
- Substack/Ghost: 1K paid subscribers at $10/mo = $10K MRR, same math as a SaaS but zero churn from software bugs

### Revenue Transparency — Does It Help or Hurt?

**It helps at early stages:**
- 94% of consumers more likely to be loyal to transparent brands (broad consumer research)
- Buffer reported 200% increase in customer acquisition after going fully transparent
- Exact MRR numbers drive far more engagement than ranges — the specificity signals confidence
- Transparency attracts early adopters who become advocates and beta testers

**It starts hurting around $10K MRR:**
- Copycats appear at this scale — they rarely win, but they create stress and capture minor market share
- Psychological cost: "when your numbers are live for the world to see, the level of stress and dread is amplified 10x" (IH community consensus)
- Security risk: disclosing significant income publicly can attract bad actors

**The emerging consensus protocol:**
- 0-$10K MRR → share everything (validation, community, distribution)
- $10K-$30K → go quiet on revenue, share product lessons only
- $30K+ → selective milestones only ("we crossed a new milestone," not the exact number)

**What always works:** Share the *how*, not just the *what*. Revenue screenshots without context are noise. "We hit $50K MRR by doing X" where X is the genuine lesson gets saved and shared.

### Content-to-Product Pipeline — How Build Logs Become Sales

The direct pipeline has three mechanisms:

**1. The ICP audience build (slow, durable)**
Create content for people who are also your users. Tony Dinh tweets for developers → developers become Typing Mind users. Pieter Levels tweets for digital nomads → nomads use Nomad List. The audience IS the customer segment.

**2. The launch announcement to warm audience (fast, event-driven)**
Build in public for 3-6 months → launch announcement gets seen by followers who already care → "I've been watching you build this" converts at 5-10x the rate of cold traffic. The IH case: 4 months of building in public → $8K MRR on day one of launch.

**3. The social proof flywheel (ongoing)**
Milestone post (e.g., "$10K MRR!") → reshares → new followers → some sign up for product → some become build-in-public content fodder ("user #1,000 just signed up from Japan") → repeat.

**Direct consulting lead generation:**
The "I built X with AI" format specifically attracts businesses that want to hire someone to build their thing. This is documented for developer content creators but less systematized. Typical pattern: YouTube video showing a build → inbound DM from business owner asking for a custom version → consulting engagement.

---

### Anti-Patterns That Turn People Off

**1. Transparency theater (wins only)**
Posting Stripe screenshots without the full context (the failures before, the ad spend, the churn) reads as bragging, not building. Audiences can tell. Failure content outperforms success content because it feels real.

**2. Building for builders, not buyers**
The build-in-public audience is largely other founders and developers. If your product is for dentists, building in public on Twitter reaches zero dentists. Works best when audience = ICP.

**3. Sporadic posting**
The algorithm rewards consistency. A founder who posts intensely for a launch then goes quiet loses most of their algorithmic reach. Daily presence is required for compounding.

**4. Revenue with no CTA**
Posting "$10K MRR!" with no link to the product or newsletter is wasted distribution. Every milestone post should have a clear next step for the reader.

**5. Going too technical too early**
Detailed engineering posts get engagement from developers but not from potential customers. The content mix should be weighted toward outcomes and journey, with technical depth as supporting evidence.

**6. Sharing too much competitive detail**
Specific acquisition tactics, conversion rate experiments, exact pricing tests — sharing these is what attracts well-resourced competitors to copy what's working. Share the revenue; protect the mechanism.

**7. Copying the format without the founder personality**
Build in public only works when the audience feels connected to a person, not a brand account. Pieter Levels succeeds because he's polarizing and authentic. Generic "product update" posts from a company handle don't work.

**8. Treating it as a short-term campaign**
Founders who try build in public for 3 months, get 200 followers, and quit are using it wrong. The timeline evidence from successful practitioners is consistently 12-24 months before meaningful audience reach.

## Actionable Takeaways

**For Misha's situation (Claude Code + multiple AI-built projects):**

1. **The format is "I built X with Claude in Y days."** This is the exact viral format right now. Jack Friks proved it. Marc Lou proved it. Misha has 10+ builds that fit this template — prediction markets, AI vision prototypes, venture pipelines, market intelligence. The content already exists as build logs.

2. **Start Twitter/X as the primary channel, today.** IndieHackers and Product Hunt are good for launch amplification but Twitter is where the compounding happens. Post 2-3x/day minimum. Engage on other builders' posts.

3. **Revenue posts are the hook, lessons are the content.** Don't just post "we hit $X." Post "we hit $X, and here's the one thing that moved the needle most." The lesson is what gets saved and shared.

4. **Build audience before building the next product.** Even 2-3 months of Twitter presence before a launch creates 3-10x better launch week numbers. The evidence from multiple creators is consistent.

5. **Newsletter as retention layer, not acquisition.** Use Twitter to grow, use a newsletter (Substack or Beehiiv) to retain. Monthly "what I shipped" email keeps warm audience engaged between launches. This is Tony Dinh's model exactly.

6. **Go quiet on exact revenue at ~$10K MRR per product.** Share milestones vaguely ("a new milestone"), keep sharing product lessons and build updates. Copycats are real but mostly fail — the real cost is psychological.

7. **ClaudeFlows is specifically well-positioned** for build-in-public content because the product IS a workflow marketplace for Claude users — the content (Claude builds) directly attracts the product's ICP.

8. **YouTube is medium-term, not day-one.** Requires more production, longer timeline. For now: repurpose Twitter threads as YouTube scripts/shorts. Build the Twitter audience first, then expand.
