# Package Pricing UX Research
**Wave 1 — ClaudeFlows Redesign**
*Research date: April 2026*

---

## Key Findings

1. **Three tiers is the sweet spot.** Two tiers force a binary choice; four or more cause decision paralysis. Three creates a natural anchor: the middle tier becomes the reference point that makes the top feel attainable and the bottom feel like a downgrade.

2. **The middle tier does the work.** Visually elevate it (raised card, accent border, "Most Popular" badge). Users arrive uncertain — social proof signals from "Most Popular" cut hesitation without pressure. The highlighted tier should be the one you actually want to sell most.

3. **CTA language maps to commitment level.** Self-serve tiers get "Get started." High-touch tiers get "Book a call" or "Talk to us." Mixing these on the same page is correct — it signals different relationships for different buyers. Never use "Learn more" as a CTA; it stalls the decision.

4. **Add-ons live below the tier grid, not inside it.** Inserting add-ons into tier cards creates noise. The pattern that works: clean 3-card grid, then a separate "Add-ons" row or section below with price per add-on and one-line description. This preserves the simplicity of the primary decision.

5. **"Best for..." copy does more than deliverables lists.** A sentence describing the ideal buyer (e.g., "Best for founders who need their first Claude workflow running this week") resolves the question "which one is for me?" faster than a checklist of 12 features.

6. **Guarantees and trust signals belong near CTAs, not in the footer.** "Pause or cancel anytime," "7-day refund," "money-back if conversion doesn't improve" — these reduce risk at the moment of decision. DesignJoy puts the 75%-back guarantee directly on the tier card.

7. **Mobile: stack cards vertically, make CTAs sticky.** Column-based grids collapse to single-card stacks on mobile. The card with the "Most Popular" badge should still appear first or second, not buried after two others. Sticky CTA bars (bottom of screen) dramatically improve mobile conversion.

8. **Price anchoring is deliberate, not accidental.** Lead with the highest-priced tier on the left or present it in a decoy position. Most agencies put their highest tier at the end, but showing the $5,000 Architecture package first makes the $500 Sprint feel instantly accessible by comparison.

9. **Checkmarks communicate inclusion; gray-out communicates exclusion.** Effective comparison grids use bold checkmarks for included features and muted gray dashes (not X marks) for excluded ones. X marks feel punitive; gray dashes feel neutral and upgrade-inviting.

10. **Annual/quarterly discounts should be visible but not mandatory.** Billing toggles (monthly/quarterly/annual) are useful for retainer products. For one-time packages like Sprint or Architecture, skip the toggle — it introduces unnecessary complexity when price is already fixed.

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. DesignJoy (designjoy.co) — Productized Design Subscription

**Current structure:** Single tier ($4,995/month, discounted from $5,995). One design request active at a time.

**Layout:** Single card with illustrated graphic, prominent price, two benefit callouts. No comparison needed; one-product clarity is a deliberate choice.

**What works:**
- The "Limited Time" discount badge creates urgency without fake countdown timers
- "Pause or cancel anytime" is a primary feature, not a footnote — it eliminates the biggest objection for high-ticket retainers
- "Get 75% back, no questions asked" is on the card, not in FAQs
- Add-on (Webflow development, $999/month) is listed separately, cleanly

**CTA:** "Join today" — action-oriented, no friction word like "subscribe"

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** The pause/cancel framing directly applies to the Retainer tier. The single add-on model is exactly how ClaudeFlows should present extras (e.g., extra skills, async support, Slack access).

---

### 2. ManyPixels (manypixels.co) — Unlimited Design Subscription

**Structure:** 4 tiers — Advanced ($599/mo), Business ($999/mo), Designated Designer ($1,299/mo — highlighted), Design Team ($2,399/mo)

**Layout:** Horizontal card columns with star icon marking the "Designated Designer" tier. Lower tiers use "Get started" CTA; higher tiers shift to "Book a call." Add-on (Webflow Development, $449/mo) appears as a clearly separate line below the grid.

**What works:**
- CTA language shift signals the relationship change: "Get started" = self-serve; "Book a call" = collaborative
- The same-day delivery promise on the highlighted tier is a concrete differentiator, not vague marketing language
- Universal features (unlimited requests, revisions, source files) are listed once at the bottom — keeps individual cards clean

**What's weak:**
- 4 tiers is one too many — Business and Designated Designer feel redundant to many buyers
- Real-time Slack communication is buried in fine print ("9AM-6PM, specific timezones only")

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** The CTA language switch is a direct pattern to adopt. Sprint → "Get started" (buy now). Architecture → "Book a call" (scoped engagement).

---

### 3. Flowout (flowout.com) — Webflow Agency Retainer

**Structure:** Tabbed layout — BUILD tab, GROW tab, HOURLY tab. Each tab shows 2-3 tiers with monthly/quarterly/yearly billing options side by side.

**Layout:** Tabs prevent overwhelm. Within each tab, billing period options appear as three columns (monthly at full price / quarterly at 10% off / yearly at 20% off). Add-ons are listed with "+ $X/month" labels.

**What works:**
- Tabbing by service type (Build / Grow / Hourly) solves the multi-product pricing problem without one massive table
- Add-on pricing is explicit: "Priority support: +$1,900/month — tasks start within 1 business day"
- Quarterly subscriptions with total-billed amount ("$26,700 billed") prevent sticker shock

**What's weak:**
- No tier is clearly highlighted as "recommended" within each tab — every option gets equal visual weight
- CTA is just "Select" — generic, no intent signaling

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** Tabbed layout is worth considering if ClaudeFlows later adds distinct service lines (Skills Marketplace vs. Done-For-You Setup vs. Retainer). For now, a single 3-card layout with add-ons below is simpler and more decisive.

---

### 4. Clarityflow (clarityflow.com) — Async Coaching Platform

**Structure:** 3 tiers — Starter ($29/mo), Professional ($79/mo), Premium ($149/mo). Monthly/yearly toggle.

**Layout:** Side-by-side cards with monthly/yearly toggle. Detailed feature comparison table below the fold. No tier highlighted as most popular.

**What works:**
- Done-for-you setup add-on is listed separately: includes "1:1 strategy session, client onboarding setup, personalized training videos" — concrete scope, no vague language
- Feature comparison table organized by functional categories (Conversations, Appointments, Commerce) rather than alphabetically

**What's weak:**
- All three tiers use identical "Get started" CTA — misses the opportunity to differentiate commitment levels
- No tier is visually elevated — adds friction to the "which one is for me" question

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** The done-for-you setup listed as an add-on (with its own concrete deliverables) is the exact model for how ClaudeFlows should present the Full Setup package when upselling from Sprint.

---

### 5. 100 Pound Social — Social Media Management Subscription

**Structure:** 3 tiers — Basic (£100/mo, 3 posts/week, 2 platforms), Premium (£170/mo, custom graphics), Pro (£255/mo, 5 posts/week + blogs/newsletters). Lead Generation as a separate add-on at £254/month.

**What works:**
- Deliverable counts (posts per week, platforms) are the primary differentiator — buyers understand units, not abstract tier names
- 20-day money-back guarantee on the card reduces risk for first-time buyers
- Add-on is positioned as a separate service line, not a feature checkbox

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** Deliverable counts work well even in consulting. "1 skill built, 1 revision round" vs "3 skills built, 3 revision rounds" is clearer than "Sprint" vs "Full Setup" alone.

---

### 6. WP Speed Fix — One-Time Developer Service

**Structure:** 3 packages — Simple Speed Optimization ($495), Audit + Consult + Implementation ($795), Technical SEO + Speed Bundle ($2,495).

**Layout:** Each package is a distinct offer, not just a tier of the same service. Price is the headline. Deliverables follow in a short list.

**What works:**
- Each package solves a different problem rather than being "more of the same"
- The step-up logic is clear: each higher package includes the prior one plus more
- No hourly rates anywhere — the fixed price is a feature in itself

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** This matches ClaudeFlows' package structure exactly. Sprint = focused fix. Full Setup = audit + implementation. Architecture = strategic engagement. The stacking logic ("each tier includes the prior") should be made explicit.

---

### 7. MobiLoud — App Conversion Service

**Structure:** Startup ($399/month + $850 setup fee), Growth ($799/month + $1,500 setup fee), Corporate (custom).

**Layout:** Two-part pricing (setup + recurring) is shown transparently, not hidden.

**What works:**
- Separating setup from recurring is honest and reduces "surprise billing" complaints
- Corporate tier uses "Contact us" not "Get started" — correctly signals the relationship shift
- User caps (1,000 active users vs. 5,000) are the primary differentiator — concrete, not marketing language

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** If ClaudeFlows ever charges a one-time setup fee alongside a Retainer, show both numbers upfront. Hiding setup costs to lower perceived price creates churn.

---

### 8. Expert LLC USA — Multi-Tier Consulting Service

**Structure:** 4 tiers — Basique ($579), Confort ($849), Premium ($2,249), Prestige ($4,000). Annual compliance bundle as recurring add-on ($899/year).

**Layout:** Each tier has a name that signals sophistication level. Premium tier includes a time guarantee ("48-hour EIN"). Top tier bundles bookkeeping into the price.

**What works:**
- The tier naming (Basique → Prestige) communicates target buyer status without spelling it out
- Time guarantee ("48-hour") on a premium tier is a concrete differentiator that justifies the price jump
- Annual add-on is separate from core service, with its own clear value proposition

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** ClaudeFlows should consider naming that maps to buyer sophistication: Sprint (for builders), Full Setup (for operators), Architecture (for CTOs/leads). The naming should reflect buyer identity, not just feature count.

### 9. Smashing Magazine Pricing UX Research — Established Patterns

Source: Smashing Magazine's comprehensive pricing page UX study.

**Lawn Mower Scan Pattern:** Users read pricing tables left-to-right, drop to the next row, move right-to-left. They frequently jump back to column headers to verify which plan they're reviewing. This means: keep plan headers sticky when the feature list is long, and keep feature labels in a fixed left column.

**Mobile Behavior:** Multi-column tables collapse to stacked cards (one per plan). Sticky bottom CTAs outperform inline CTAs on mobile. Dropdown "compare plans" selectors help when users want to evaluate only two of three options on a small screen.

**Progressive Disclosure:** Lead with 5-7 decision-critical features in the cards. Move the full comparison table below the fold. Users completing a second focused scan benefit from the full table; first-time scanners are overwhelmed by it.

**Feature Absence:** Show exclusion with gray dashes, not X marks. X marks feel adversarial; dashes feel like a neutral "not yet."

---

### 10. Ubunzo Studio — Ticket-Style Horizontal Card Pricing

**Layout:** Unconventional. Tiers presented as horizontal ticket-style cards with tabs, not vertical columns. Each tab corresponds to a tier; clicking reveals that tier's scope.

**What works:**
- Tabs reduce visual comparison fatigue — users explore one tier at a time instead of scanning across all simultaneously
- "Best value" callout on one tier, with a description of "type of projects that benefit most"
- CTA: "Book This Plan" — specific, action-oriented, includes the word "plan" to confirm the selection

**Relevance to ClaudeFlows:** For a consulting service with meaningfully different deliverables per tier, the tab-per-tier model could reduce cognitive load. Worth A/B testing against the standard side-by-side card layout.

## Actionable Takeaways

### For ClaudeFlows Package Pricing Page

**Structure**
- Use exactly 3 tiers: Sprint ($500), Full Setup ($2,000), Architecture ($5,000+)
- Position Full Setup as the highlighted "Most Popular" tier — it's the realistic anchor for most buyers
- Add a 4th row below the grid for the Retainer ($500-1K/mo) as a standalone section with its own CTA, clearly labeled as "ongoing" vs. the one-time tiers above

**Card Design**
- Each card: tier name + one-sentence buyer description → price (large, no hiding) → 5-7 deliverables (specific, countable) → delivery timeframe → CTA button
- Highlight Full Setup card with elevated shadow, accent border, and "Most Popular" badge
- Sprint and Architecture cards use slightly muted background, not identical styling

**CTA Language by Tier**
- Sprint: "Get started" → links directly to purchase/checkout
- Full Setup: "Get started" → links to checkout or short intake form
- Architecture: "Book a call" → links to calendar (Calendly/Cal.com)
- Retainer: "Apply" or "Let's talk" → intake form to qualify fit

**Deliverable Copy**
- Be specific and countable: "2 Claude skills built and deployed," "1 architecture review session (60 min)," "async Slack support for 30 days"
- Include the timeline: "Delivered in 5 business days," "Scoped call within 48 hours"
- Add one guarantee per tier: Sprint → "Works or we rebuild it." Full Setup → "30-day async support included." Architecture → "Written decision log delivered."

**Add-Ons Section**
- Place below the 3-card grid, above the FAQ
- Format: Add-on name | Price | One-line description | "Add to any plan" button
- Example add-ons: Extra skill +$200, Async Slack support +$300/mo, Rush delivery (48hr) +$150

**Price Anchoring**
- Show Architecture ($5,000+) last but reference it early in the page headline: "From $500 sprints to $5,000 architecture engagements"
- This sets the range expectation so Sprint reads as accessible, not cheap

**Comparison Table (Below the Fold)**
- Rows: deliverables, included revisions, delivery time, support channel, async access, source files, follow-up calls
- Columns: Sprint | Full Setup | Architecture
- Use bold checkmark for included, gray dash for not included
- No X marks

**Trust Signals (Near CTAs)**
- "Fixed price. No hourly billing. No scope creep."
- "Pause or cancel the Retainer anytime."
- Short testimonial or outcome stat ("3 teams shipped in week 1") near the Full Setup CTA

**Mobile**
- Stack cards: Full Setup → Sprint → Architecture (lead with the anchor)
- Sticky CTA bar at bottom showing the most popular package price
- Comparison table becomes a horizontal scroll table or collapsed accordion on mobile

**What to Avoid**
- No hourly rate equivalents ("That's $125/hour!") — undermines the fixed-price value prop
- No more than 4 tiers including Retainer
- No vague CTA language: "Learn more," "Inquire," "Explore"
- No features listed as checkboxes inside the card if they're the same across all tiers — move universal features to a "all plans include" block at the bottom
