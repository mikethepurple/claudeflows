# Name Validation: "Curated Flows" for Non-Technical Business Leaders

Research date: 2026-04-17
Verdict: **The name has real problems. Not fatal, but worth understanding before you build a brand around it.**

---

## 1. Do Non-Technical Leaders Use the Word "Flows"?

**Short answer: No. "Flows" is platform jargon, not CEO language.**

"Flows" is the native terminology of automation platforms:
- Microsoft renamed "Microsoft Flow" to "Power Automate" in 2019 specifically to be clearer to business users, but kept "flows" as the internal unit name (cloud flows, desktop flows, business process flows)
- Activepieces, n8n, and other platforms use "flows" as the technical term for automation sequences
- Zapier deliberately avoided "flows" and branded their units as "Zaps" — a consumer-friendly word

The people who say "flows" are the people who BUILD automations: citizen developers, ops managers, technical marketers. The people who BUY automations (your target: CEOs, founders, dept heads) say:

- "I need to **systematize** my delivery"
- "We're doing everything **manually** and it's killing our margins"
- "I need someone to just **set it up** for me"
- "How do I **scale without adding headcount**?"
- "Fix the process first, then automate it"
- "Shift repetitive tasks to machines"
- "Work smarter, experiment faster"

(These phrases come directly from your own buyer persona research in 05-buyer-personas.md and from CEO interviews in Make.com, McKinsey, and SHRM surveys.)

The vocabulary of the buyer is **outcomes and problems**: save time, cut costs, stop doing it manually, scale. Not "flows," not "workflows," not "automations." Those are solution words. CEOs buy solutions described in their language, not yours.

**Risk:** A CEO hears "Curated Flows" and thinks either (a) it's a design tool, (b) it's for developers, or (c) they need to Google what it means. None of those are good first impressions.

---

## 2. Does "Curated" Signal Premium, or Hipster?

**"Curated" was voted the #1 worst jargon word of 2020 by The Buzzsaw.** The judges called it "a word that has been brutalised by Hipster culture."

The problem is context-dependent:
- **"Curated" works for:** content collections, event experiences, product selections (DTC boxes, playlists)
- **"Curated" doesn't work for:** B2B services sold to time-pressed executives. It sounds like effort and taste, not results and speed.

A developer describing a remodeled shopping center as "a curated center with a thoughtful tenant mix" literally made people roll their eyes. The word triggers skepticism in business contexts.

For your specific audience (non-technical CEOs buying AI automation services), "curated" creates a mismatch:
- It suggests **selection** (choosing from options) when your value prop is **done-for-you execution**
- It suggests **aesthetic refinement** when your buyers want **operational results**
- It sounds like a Substack newsletter about AI tools, not a service that automates your invoicing pipeline

The word isn't dead, but it has heavy baggage. In a B2B context where trust and clarity matter most, it adds cognitive load rather than reducing it.

## 3. What Words DO Non-Technical Leaders Actually Use?

From CEO surveys (McKinsey, Gartner, Deloitte, SHRM) and your existing buyer persona research, the real vocabulary clusters around:

| What They Say | What They Mean |
|---|---|
| "We need to **automate** this" | Generic — everyone says this |
| "I need someone to **set it up**" | Done-for-you, implementation |
| "**Systematize** our operations" | Process, not one-off |
| "**Streamline** the business" | Efficiency, less friction |
| "**Scale without hiring**" | Growth without headcount |
| "**Just make it work**" | Frustration with complexity |
| "Save my team **10-15 hours/week**" | Concrete time savings |
| "I don't have time to **figure it out**" | Delegation, expertise |
| "**Someone who knows what they're doing**" | Trust, competence |

The pattern: their language is about **delegation to competent people** and **concrete outcomes**. Not about the mechanism (flows, workflows, automations) and not about aesthetics (curated, crafted, bespoke).

## 4. Alternative Positioning Angles Tested

| Angle | Verdict |
|---|---|
| "AI automation that works out of the box" | Clear but generic. Sounds like SaaS, not a service. No human element. |
| "Done-for-you AI automation" | **Strongest.** Matches exactly how buyers describe what they want ("I need someone to just set it up"). But it's a tagline, not a brand name. |
| "Your AI automation team" | Good — positions as people, not software. "Team" implies ongoing relationship. But sounds like an agency landing page, not a brand. |
| "AI workflows for your business" | "Workflows" is slightly better than "flows" (Microsoft and Zapier use it) but still solution-language, not buyer-language. |
| "We automate your business with AI" | Too blunt, too broad. Could be anything from a chatbot to a warehouse robot. No premium signal. |

**Best positioning formula based on research:** "Done-for-you" framing + specific outcome + "no code/no hassle" qualifier. Example from your own buyer persona research: *"Your agency team spends 15 hours/week on reporting. I'll automate it in 48 hours."*

## 5. How Successful B2B Service Brands Name Themselves

Patterns from B2B naming research (Bluetext, Clarity Quest, Velocity Partners, DeSantis Breindel):

**What works for non-technical buyers:**
- **Descriptive names** that say what you do: Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow, HubSpot
- **Outcome names** that promise a result: Rippling, Calm, Notion, Slack
- **Three syllables or fewer** (Sony, Target, Stripe, Gusto)
- **Names that work as verbs or actions** ("Let's Slack about it," "Just Stripe it")

**What struggles:**
- Compound words that require decoding ("CuratedFlows" = two concepts to parse)
- Names where both parts are abstract ("curated" + "flows" = no concrete anchor)
- Names that could be anything (a newsletter? a playlist app? a yoga studio?)

**Successful AI automation agency names in practice:**
- Brave Bison (memorable, no jargon)
- 10Clouds (short, punchy)
- Altar.io (evocative, simple)
- NoGood (contrarian, memorable)
- Make.com (verb, simple, clear)

None of the successful ones explain what they do in the name. They're either short + memorable or descriptive + clear. "Curated Flows" sits in an awkward middle — it tries to be descriptive but describes the mechanism, not the outcome.

## 6. The Three Tests

### "Say it on the phone" test
"Our website is curated flows dot com."
- Will people hear "curated" or "curate it"?
- Will they type "curatedflows" or "curated-flows" or "curated flows"?
- **Verdict: Marginal pass.** Not terrible, but not clean. Two words compounds always have the hyphen/space ambiguity.

### "Type it from memory" test
Someone saw your ad 3 days ago. Can they find you?
- They might Google "curated flows AI" — that's specific enough to find you
- They might Google "curated automation" or "AI automation curated" — less certain
- They will NOT Google "curated flows" from memory. The name doesn't stick because neither word is distinctive enough.
- **Verdict: Fail.** The name is forgettable. Both words are common and generic. Compare: "NoGood" or "Make" — you'd remember those.

### "CEO sees it in an ad" test
A CEO scrolls past "Curated Flows" on LinkedIn. What do they think you sell?
- Best case: "Something about AI workflows? Maybe worth clicking."
- Likely case: "Sounds like a design tool or a playlist service."
- Worst case: "Developer thing. Not for me."
- **Verdict: Fail.** The name does not communicate "we automate your business" to a non-technical buyer. It communicates "we're in the automation/workflow space" to people who already know that space.

---

## Brutally Honest Summary

**"Curated Flows" is a name that makes sense to you (the builder) but not to your buyer (the CEO).**

The problems:
1. **"Flows"** is automation-platform jargon. CEOs don't use it.
2. **"Curated"** has hipster/buzzword baggage and signals selection, not execution.
3. **Together**, they describe HOW you work (you curate automation flows), not WHAT the buyer gets (their business runs without them).
4. The name is **forgettable** — two common words that don't create a distinctive mental image.
5. It **sounds like a tool**, not a service. CEOs buying done-for-you services want to feel like they're hiring people, not subscribing to software.

**What's not wrong:**
- The domain is clean and available — that's real value
- The name isn't offensive, confusing, or unpronounceable
- It could work as a sub-brand or product name (e.g., "the Curated Flows library" within a broader service brand)

---

## Recommendations

**Option A: Keep the domain, change the brand positioning.** Use CuratedFlows.com as the website but brand the service differently. The service name appears in ads, social, and conversations. The domain just needs to be findable. Many successful companies have domain names that differ slightly from their spoken brand (Stripe's domain is stripe.com but they call themselves "Stripe" not "Stripe Payments").

**Option B: Use CuratedFlows as the storefront name, but lead with outcome-driven taglines in all marketing.** Never let "Curated Flows" stand alone. Always pair it with "Done-for-you AI automation" or "We automate your business so you can scale." The name becomes the container; the tagline does the selling.

**Option C: Rebrand before launch.** If you haven't built significant brand equity yet, consider names that pass all three tests. The domain investment is sunk cost — don't let it anchor a suboptimal brand decision.

**Names worth exploring (if rebranding):**
- Something with "auto" or "automate" for instant clarity
- Something outcome-focused: what does the buyer GET?
- Something with a human/team signal: buyers want to hire people, not subscribe to tools
- Three syllables max, easy to spell, works as a verb if possible
